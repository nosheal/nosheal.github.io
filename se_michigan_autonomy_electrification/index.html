Southeast Michigan: the automotive stack

Newlab · Sector map · July 2026

Key takeaways — Southeastern Michigan can uniquely accelerate the development of autonomous, electrified vehicles.

This region provides comprehensive assets across the full stack of the development and deployment cycle for autonomous electrified vehicles. This report provides a detailed overview of this stack, and how these assets can compress the timeline from design to a validated vehicle on the road:

  1. 1
    Access to autonomy and ADAS talent

    63 named development-and-engineering centers and 13 research centers sit on an engineering-services base of 79,237 jobs — nearly twice the national share within a curated knowledge-services basket (LQ 1.9; basket defined under About the data).

  2. 2
    Access to proving grounds and autonomous testing infrastructure

    24 validation and proving facilities plus 9 instrumented public testbeds, with the added benefit of enabling testing and validation at both early prototyping stages and in real-world environments.

  3. 3
    Access to suppliers up and down the identified stack, especially in the electrification value chain (power electronics, electric powertrains, battery production)

    A 2,702-plant supplier base with 118 power-electronics facilities, anchored by development centers like Eaton's traction-inverter operation in Southfield, Vitesco's power electronics in Auburn Hills, and American Axle's e-Drive centers — alongside 25 named battery-chain plants and 240 plants carrying EV/AV capability.

  4. 4
    Ability to go from design to validated vehicle in the same geography — with ecosystem partners providing white-glove access to customers and helping derisk entry

    190 named assets span all seven stages in one region — a company designs with the engineering base, sources from the supplier network, validates at the proving grounds, deploys on public testbeds, and sells to the OEMs whose plants sit in the same counties — and 27 coordinating partners, including Newlab at Michigan Central and the Office of Future Mobility and Electrification, broker that sequence into programmed entry.

Southeast Michigan is known as the place that builds the cars. The region holds 298,984 manufacturing jobs across 5,278 establishments; 43.5% of those jobs sit in core motor-vehicle and parts work. This auto-sector employment is just the tip of the capabilities that are represented in SE Michigan. This report unpacks the full stack of capabilities present in the region, establishing the region as a unique hub that combines research, design and engineering, testing, validation, suppliers, assembly, deployment and operations, and the broader ecosystem. Southeastern Michigan documents named assets at every one of these stages — a full stack, from research to field operation.

The report dives into each of these stages, documenting key institutions, firms, and capabilities found across Southeastern Michigan.

L1 · Research & Discovery

Where the vehicle is still being invented

13 named research assets sit in Southeastern Michigan — the University of Michigan’s transportation and battery institutes, the Army’s Ground Vehicle Systems Center, Toyota’s North American research institute, and more. These research assets combine industry-funded R&D with public R&D at leading universities, including $1.1B obligated across 249 federal awards, FY20–26, to recipients or work performed in these counties.

This R&D hub is anchored by large corporate R&D centers. For example, General Motors’ IRA-era EV-conversion grant brought in 500 million dollars in a single obligation. Of the $1.1B obligated, $591M funds research proper; the balance is plant-conversion and demonstration assistance (DOE CFDA 81.253), which the GM grant dominates. This corporate R&D base is supported by strong university-led programs: the University of Michigan alone carries 158 million dollars across 100 awards, the deepest sustained research presence in the region. Corporate recipients hold 675 million; universities together hold 214 million across 149 awards.

Federal research funding by institution, FY20–26

By technology, the book splits three ways: software-defined mobility draws $1.0B, electrification $0.9B, and autonomy $0.2B; an award can carry more than one technology tag, so the three figures overlap and do not sum to the total.

About this number

The autonomy figure is a low-end bound. It misses Other Transaction Agreements (OTAs), the dominant federal funding vehicle for defense-tech and autonomy R&D, used routinely by DIU, DARPA, and the Army’s DEVCOM GVSC, which sits in this footprint. This extraction can show a false or artificially low federal figure for an autonomy asset that in fact holds substantial OTA funding.

L3 · Validation & Proving

Where the design is proven under control

24 validation and proving facilities answer a single question: does the technology work? These are the closed, controlled, instrumented environments, including the gated proving ground, a mock city, battery-abuse cells, and more. Mcity at the University of Michigan, for example, is a purpose-built fake town for testing automated vehicles without traffic.

When considering autonomy and electrification, testing under controlled conditions is only half of the problem. Many assets included in this layer also support deployment in less controlled settings (currently mapped in detail in L6). The University of Michigan runs Mcity as a closed course and supports connected-vehicle deployments on Ann Arbor’s public streets. The region holds both the controlled proving ground and the real-world testbed for the same technology at once.

L4 · Component Supply Base

Where the parts are made

Every point is one automotive-relevant supplier plant, colored by its primary NAICS group and clickable for the firm, its six-digit NAICS code, and its county. The base is a metalworking and machining economy. Of the 2,702 plants, 1,130 belong to the core motor-vehicle and parts codes, 701 do machining and coating work, 492 do stamping and metal forming, and 435 work in plastics and polymers. Batteries are the smallest slice at 34 plants. A plant can hold more than one capability, so the slices overlap and do not sum to the total.

This is the widest layer in the report and the one the assembly plants actually run on. The named inventory here is narrow by design — 25 named battery-chain plants, anchored on the NAATBatt battery census (which covers batteries only), within 27 named component-manufacturing assets overall. Behind them is the real supply base: 2,702 automotive-relevant supplier plants across the eleven counties, mapped below.

Within that base, 118 plants fall in a power-electronics code set carried over from our Long Island power-electronics study — the electric-machinery codes (motors, transformers, switchgear, relays) plus battery manufacturing. That is a different NAICS code set from the 206 plants in the broader vehicle-electronics and semiconductor group (printed-circuit assembly, semiconductors, motor-vehicle electrical equipment); the two are counted from distinct code lists and overlap in only a handful of facilities.

About this number

What a point means, and does not. Inclusion is by NAICS — a plant is here because it operates in one of the bundle’s automotive-relevant codes, not because its automotive work is confirmed. Counts are registry-presence, not confirmed-active production; roughly three in five rows carry no active-status flag, and the registry retains some stale and closed sites. The membership slices overlap — a plant can match several — so the per-group counts do not sum to the 2,702-plant total.

L5 · Vehicle Assembly

Where the vehicle is put together

12 final-assembly plants operate in the footprint. Assembly plants are large and public; the inventory here is close to complete. What the plants are building, on the other hand, is a moving target.

The powertrain transition is not a straight line, and the plant record shows it plainly. Of the 12 plants, only 7 carry a documented powertrain plan; 5 do not. Among the documented, exactly one — GM Factory ZERO — is a pure battery-electric plant. Ford’s Rouge EV Center is EV today but transitioning to extended-range (EREV). Stellantis runs mixed ICE-and-plug-in lines. And two GM plants, Orion and Lansing Grand River, have had EV plans reversed back to combustion.

L6 · Deployment & Operations

Where the technology meets the public road

Deployment is the open, public, operational world — the live corridor, the connected street, the autonomous service in traffic. It answers a harder question than the proving ground does: not whether the technology works, but whether it survives reality. 24 deployments sit in the footprint, 9 of them instrumented testbeds. Three run as physical road corridors rather than points — Cavnue’s I-94 connected-and-automated-vehicle lane, the Planet M / TARDEC connected corridor on I-69, and Electreon’s wireless-charging stretch of Michigan Avenue — and the map draws them as the road segments they are.

The die-off is part of the record, and the honest version keeps it in view. Argo AI ran a Ford-backed self-driving test fleet on metro Detroit roads from 2019 and was disbanded in 2022 when Ford and Volkswagen withdrew. L7 · Supporting and Coordinating Ecosystem

Where a company grows

The last layer is the scaffolding that turns a working prototype into a company: 27 accelerators, venture funds, workforce programs, and industry bodies. MICHauto and the MEMA supplier division convene the incumbents; MEDC, the Office of Future Mobility, and PlanetM run the state’s mobility programs; the community colleges run EV and battery technician training. A startup that lands here can reach a research base, an engineering workforce, a proving ground, a supplier network, an assembly footprint, and a public-road testbed — the whole stack — from one place.

Methodology & sources

Sources. Named assets: desk research plus the NAATBatt/NREL battery supply-chain database. Supplier base: the Manufacturing Facility Registry (see manf.us) cut to the automotive-relevant NAICS bundle, geocoded to the eleven-county footprint. Employment and establishment figures: the anchor-supplemented CBP corrected layer, 2023. Research funding: USASpending prime contracts and assistance, FY20–26, recipient or place of performance in the footprint. Every number the page renders resolves from a single data file at build time; there are no hand-entered figures.

About this number

Over- and undercounts. We undercount small, low-visibility shops in the named-asset layers because desk research finds institutions with a press and corporate web presence first. We overcount the supplier base because the firm registry retains some stale and closed sites, so registry-presence counts sit above confirmed-active reality. Assembly plants are large and visible; that layer is close to complete.

About this number

Facility is not establishment. A supplier point is a registry facility record, not a Census establishment, and inclusion is by NAICS code, not confirmed automotive work. Counts are registry-presence; roughly three in five rows carry no active-status flag.

About this number

What the engineering-services location quotient is measured against. The engineering-services LQ (1.9) is a within-services quotient: the region’s share of engineering and testing employment (NAICS 541330 engineering services, 541370 surveying and mapping, 541380 testing laboratories) relative to the nation’s, measured inside a curated seven-indicator knowledge-services basket — computer systems design (5415), data processing and hosting (518210), specialized design (541420), engineering and testing (541330, 541370, 541380), scientific and technical consulting (541690), scientific R&D (541715), and software publishers (511210). It is not an economy-wide or all-of-professional-services quotient; a value of 1 would mean the region holds the same share of this seven-indicator basket as the nation does. Source: County Business Patterns non-manufacturing indicators, 2023.

About this number

Other Transaction Agreements are invisible here. USASpending does not capture OTAs, the main federal vehicle for defense and autonomy R&D. Federal funding for autonomy assets is understated, sometimes to zero. Never read a low or absent figure as ‘no federal funding’ for an autonomy asset.

About this number

Statewide plants outside the ring. EV and battery plants beyond the eleven counties — BlueOval in Marshall, LG in Holland — are shown only as statewide context and are not counted in any footprint total.

About this number

Single-year cross-sections. Employment and establishment figures are reported for one year and not compared across the 2016–17 Census methodology break, which introduced artificial discontinuities at the industry level.

About this data — caveats, coverage, provenance

Employee figures

Employee counts are directional only. Company-level reporting coverage is partial; sums are over reporting member companies, not confirmed firm totals.

Known biases (selection / geography / recency)

  • **Selection bias**: Large, well-known institutions (OEMs, top-tier suppliers, major universities) are reliably found; small/independent engineering shops and Tier-2/3 suppliers are systematically under-represented because web-search discovery favors entities with press coverage or corporate web presence. Several file1 "excluded_or_corrected_candidates" entries (Belcan, Bertrandt, LTTS, Cyient, Qualcomm Auburn Hills) were dropped for exactly this reason — not because they don't exist.
  • **Geographic bias**: English-language, US-facing web presence is required for an asset to surface; source-file notes explicitly document zero Chinese OEM outposts and no verified Chinese-owned engineering presence beyond Yanfeng — this could be a true absence or a discovery-method blind spot; not distinguishable from this corpus alone.
  • **Recency bias**: A meaningful share of employment/headcount figures are explicitly flagged "dated" (2017, 2022, 2023 vintage) even where more recent facts (status, product mix, expansions) were found; figures should not be treated as current without a fresh check.
  • **Reporting bias**: Most figures (headcounts, investment amounts, membership counts) are self-reported via corporate press releases or trade press, not independently audited.
  • **Survivor bias**: Ended AV pilots and demonstrations (May Mobility original Bedrock pilot, Argo AI, Refraction AI, Navya Brush Park, 3M/MDOT work zone) were deliberately retained and labeled `status: ended` rather than dropped, specifically to avoid survivor bias in the L6 layer.
  • **Language bias**: Discovery relies entirely on English-language sources; see geographic bias note above.
  • **Consolidation-introduced bias**: Because 3 independently-run desk-research passes fed this corpus, sourcing depth is uneven across layers depending on which pass covered them (e.g., L4 was never a dedicated pass — see segment coverage gap above).
